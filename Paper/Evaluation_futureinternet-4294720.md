Reviewer 1
This study proposes a unified analytical framework integrating information theory, representation learning, and constrained optimization, linking the theoretical and practical perspectives of AI-native PHY.
1. Please explicitly disclose all benchmark code, data generation scripts, model weights, training hyperparticipant random seeds in supplementary materials or the repository. If confidentiality prevents disclosure, please provide a complete reproduction recipe (hardware, epochs, batch, loss, frame-error count, etc.).
2. Please supplement with systematic experiments on channel distribution shifts (AWGN→Rayleigh/Rician, different Dopplers, noise/interference models), quantize performance degradation (BER/NMSE/PSNR), and provide empirical results for domain-adaptation or online-adaptation.
3. Please compile test results of the main architectures (autoencoder, Transformer, DetNet, U-Net, Diffusion) on the specified hardware using unified metrics (FLOPs, number of parameters, quantized INT8 latency, energy/bit pJ), demonstrating their feasibility in URLLC/real-time applications.

Reviewer 2
Comments and Suggestions for Authors
The manuscript addresses a timely and relevant topic, AI-native physical-layer design for future 6G systems. Its broad coverage of neural channel coding, channel estimation, signal detection, beamforming, semantic communications, implementation aspects, and standardization issues is valuable. The attempt to connect information theory, representation learning, and end-to-end physical-layer optimization is also appreciated. However, the manuscript requires major revision before publication.
1. The claims on the performance benefits of AI-native PHY should be moderated. The paper’s own autoencoder benchmark does not convincingly support the claimed performance advantages. In several short-block configurations, the autoencoder does not reach the target BER within the tested SNR range and performs significantly worse than the Polar-code baseline. Since the authors attribute this mainly to the limited CPU-only training budget, the benchmark should either be substantially strengthened with fair and statistically reliable comparisons, or clearly reframed as a limited proof-of-concept experiment rather than evidence of performance superiority.
2. The standardization discussion should be carefully corrected and updated. Although the manuscript briefly mentions TR 38.843 and Release 18/19 standardization, it does not adequately cover the actual scope and status of the 3GPP RAN1 Rel-19 Work Item on AI/ML for NR air interface (NR_AIML_air). The authors should clearly distinguish between the Rel-18 RAN1 Study Item documented in TR 38.843 and the subsequent Rel-19 Work Item. In particular, the manuscript should discuss the Rel-19 scope, including normative work on the one-sided AI/ML general framework, AI/ML-based beam management, and AI/ML-based positioning, as well as the continued study of two-sided modeling, CSI feedback, data collection, model transfer/delivery, testing, and interoperability. Technical Reports should not be presented as normative specifications, and the manuscript should avoid implying that native AI PHY has already been established as a mandatory 6G design requirement.
3. Several mathematical and technical claims should be made more precise. In particular, the finite-blocklength discussion, autoencoder optimality claims, complexity comparisons, and comparisons between neural and conventional schemes require more careful qualification.
4. The manuscript needs editorial improvement. Some terminology, grammar, table formatting, figure quality, and section titles should be revised. The authors should also distinguish more clearly between their own results, prior literature results, and speculative future directions.
Overall, the manuscript has potential as a broad review of AI-native PHY for 6G, but it should be revised into a more balanced, technically accurate, and evidence-supported survey.

Comments on the Quality of English Language
1.	Expressions such as “Very High (complete)” in Table 1 are not appropriate for an objective academic comparison and should be replaced with more neutral and verifiable descriptions.
2.	Some sentences are overly long and should be divided for clarity, especially in the Introduction and Standardization sections.
3.	The manuscript should be checked for consistent use of terms such as “AI-native PHY,” “AI/ML air interface,” “physical layer,” “air interface,” and “Future Internet.”
4.	Several tables contain formatting issues, awkward line breaks, or inconsistent capitalization, which should be corrected before publication.
